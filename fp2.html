My translation, including syntactic and rhyme transformation

Thus Love comes to look upon this debris,
Nostrils replaced by these black cavities,
These lips plough as a furrow sulfurous,
By this gag-like tongue swollen and bulbous,
This forehead by pustules unseemly lit,
These eyelids with no eyes and eyes with no lids,
Discouraging scenes! whose reality
Leaves its black mark on the horrified eye,
Their simulacra in wax, the faded sight,
Pass through our minds during each sleepless night,
their pallid portraits-- this image alone
Bristles our hairs and chills us to the bone.





Accompanying Essay on my Translation

I revisited a text I had translated for an undergraduate essay: “Syphilis” by Auguste Barthelemy (1851), an epic poem about the dangers, symptoms, prevention, and triumph over this venereal disease that plagued urban France in the 19th century. In later editions of this poem (reprinted 4 times, nearly doubling in length each time) the poem’s publication also included the intervention of a certified doctor who discussed the poem’s content medically as a type of intertextual addendum. Barthelemy also was hugely influenced by a similar epic poem by Fracastoro, also entitled “Syphilis” published in Latin centuries earlier in 1530. Barthelemy often refers to Fracastor in the first “song” of his epic poem, likely imitating some verses. Fracastor’s version has been retranslated many times. My first “translation” of selected stanzas was merely a transliteration for the purposes of an Environmental Studies class. I rendered it literally, word-for-word, getting the sense across in comprehensible English, paying no time or attention to rhythm, form, sonic qualities, etc. I wanted to revisit it and pay attention to its musical quality. The original French is almost perfect alexandrine, the 12-syllable line, but 12 syllables sounds a little unwieldy in English (which is dominated by much sharper sounds than the soft and fluid French). Pound justifies “dropping a couple of syllables per line” to arrive at an equivalency of naturalness to the rhythm in the original and in English. Feeling this true of French as well, I wrote this with 10 syllables per line.

Even though I personally would choose not to translate rhyme, since I believe content is more crucial than sound, Pound was an advocate for maintaining the musicality of the poem. For him, there is no poem without its rhythm. “Equivalency” does not matter so much as “correlation,” especially when that correlation serves to convey the sonic qualities in the poem. Thus, I did my best to give English rhyme to this poem, even though it meant contorting some of the sentences and affecting the literal “word-for-word” translation, which is what is produced when we run something through a Machine Translation. One example is the word “bâillon” which means “gag”, which as a mono-syllabic end-rhyme has very little poetic quality in English. I turned the idea behind the noun “gag” into the gerund “gagging” (to produce an effect of actually gagging on the hard g sounds in gagging and tongue) and then, like bâillon has within it the words for ball and balloon, used bulbous as an indicator of shape (which conveniently rhymes well-enough with sulfurous). In one place, I actually switched two clauses, “their simulacra in wax” and “their memory alone” in order to maintain the syllables per line and find effective English rhymes. I tried to enact Pound’s concept of proportionate antiquity this poem translation, which, although written in comprehensible “modern” French, has certain qualities that date it as 19th century. I did not always maintain the syntactical structure, but when I changed it I attempted to make it sound “older.” Words like “plough” with the British spelling, wax simulacra (which could be simply “wax sculpture” but brings to mind hokey wax museums), and “pustules” (instead of pimples or boils) render this version completely readable but outside of our 21st century every day diction. It is also crucial that this is not an isolated text, but is situated in a system involving Fracastoro and all his translations. According to Pound, maintaining the genealogy of a text is crucial to a successful translation. Barthelemey’s poem should perceived as more modern than a translation of Fracastoro if put side-by-side. Obviously the scope of a proportionate translation of the two epics and their subsequent translations is beyond that of this assignment, but it’s worth considering its effects on this excerpt in terms proportionate antiquity.
