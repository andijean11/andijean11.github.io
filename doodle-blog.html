EXPERIMENTAL TRANSLATIONS

July 17, 2017

As I begin the sixth week of this fellowship, I'm starting to hit a groove! I've learned a lot of coding strategies. The first week I had ideas and I was trying to pick and choose bits of code that would accomodate, but now that I've dedicated much more time to learning a large breadth of coding, I can take what I want to do and think of multiple ways to accomplish it.

This is especially important when working on my experimental translations. In addition to exploring translation theory by exposing the two languages (original and target) to the reader in an interactive way, I believe that digital publishing has the capability to produce interactive text that isn't possible on paper. This is more similar to E-Poetry, and I'm trying to bring it to translation.

That's why I decided to incorporate Laura Moser's translations into this first stage of my website. She is also a student here at the UIowa MFA in Literary Translation program. Her translations of Ancient Greek and Latin poetry incorporate imagination and ingenuity. She focuses on bringing the translation-reading experience onto the page. I feel there is a lot of potential in her work and in other works like this if we open it up to the internet.

Of course, experimental digital translation is not my invention. Two notable websites include http://www.yhchang.com/ and www.85bawu.com. YHChang uses flash animation to control the speed of the text, manipulating the reader's experience. And The 85 Project uses art across media to bring a physical existence to translations. What if translations were art instead of text?

I hope my website offers another space for experimental translations. Without going so far as 3D printing algorithms into benches, I hope these poems invite readers to expand their mindset about what the process of translation is and what translations can look like.

CODING
July 12, 2017

Hello again blog readers! Although I know that's a bit gratuitous since, as of now, this website is only accessible by me because it's saved locally on a harddrive. So I'm being a bit silly.

I've been learning so much coding my head hurts. As per my sister's recommendation, I used Codecademy to review html, JavaScript, and CSS, and to learn Bootstrap and JQuery for the first time. I really enjoy the free lessons that teach you how to code. If you do the 'building a website' one, you'll probably notice that my website looks very similar, at least in this early stage. Codecademy gave me the tools to read a code and understand everything it was doing, instead of blindly cutting and pasting a template.

I was introduced to coding just by being a preteen in the early 2000s; you needed small amounts of html to customize your myspace page (and my Neopets shop!). I did online role-play storytelling with other teens and how else do you customize your font except with html? However, that was about the extent of it.

In college I took intro to Comp. Science where they taught us Scheme, this verify old and recursive language. Here's some code I'm still proud of that turns any given image into black and white, because I got extra credit for its consision:

(define monochrome (lambda (image lens-color) (image-variant image (lambda (rgb) (let ((modified-rgb-components-sum (+ (* 0.30 (rgb-red rgb)) (* 0.59 (rgb-green rgb)) (* 0.11 (rgb-blue rgb))))) (rgb-list->rgb (map (o (l-s * 0.5) (l-s + modified-rgb-components-sum)) (rgb->rgb-list lens-color))))))))

The professor said I had an aptitude for coding but that was about the end of it. I had to learn a little php for the employee portal I managed as a student manager, but again, that's about it.

Learning how to build this website has been rewarding and frustrating. I know that it has a lot of bugs. If one day this website is actually worth its salt, maybe I'll go back and fix those things, but for now, it serves its purpose.

Soon I'll get some actual doodles on here, because I'm a very visual thinker, and I believe in the power of comics to make complicated ideas accessible.

THOUGHTS ON DIGITAL TRANSLATION

June 29, 2017

Hello blog readers! I’m Andrea, one of the fellows in the UIowa Digital Scholarship & Publishing Studio this summer. I’m in the middle of the MFA in Literary Translation Program at UIowa, and I translate from French to English. This summer I’m working on a website that includes some translations with digital features, inviting the reader to think like a translator and explore how translators make decisions. For this post, I’m going to throw out some ideas that I’ve been mulling around in my head regarding Machine Translation.I could conduct research and practice digital translation for the next ten years and still not be done, and the research from my first year would become obsolete by the end of the decade. This may seem daunting, but for me, young and naïve 23-year-old academic who still doesn’t drink caffeine, the potential of this field is exciting.

I’ve been reading a lot of theory recently about machine translation and technology, specifically Google Translate which has gained dominance in the last five years. I love the ways these articles/essays both underscore and undermine traditional translation theory. Translation theory is thus incredibly dynamic, constantly altered by the technologies that necessitate and facilitate its existence.

The field of translation is growing, and so is the fear that one day we translators will be completely replaced by Translation Machines. If you’re not scared yet, read about Google Translate’s new “Neural Machine,” which debuted last fall and is gaining traction in new languages. A brief quote that explains how it works:

“The team trained its system on hundreds of hours of Spanish audio with corresponding English text. In each case, it used several layers of neural networks – computer systems loosely modelled on the human brain – to match sections of the spoken Spanish with the written translation. To do this, it analysed the waveform of the Spanish audio to learn which parts seemed to correspond with which chunks of written English. When it was then asked to translate, each neural layer used this knowledge to manipulate the audio waveform until it was turned into the corresponding section of written English.”
In other words, it’s translating “wave-to-wave” instead of “word-to-word.” This is a translation on a neurological level, not just a transcription of bilingual dictionaries. The emphasis is on accuracy of meaning without the obstacle of the words themselves.

And, if you thought that Google had the monopoly on machine translation, just this week Amazon announced working on its own Machine Translation services.

I entered this program at UIowa because it seemed like a perfect fit for my interests in French literature. But will the skills I gain here be superseded by Machine Translation? Will my career be safe (if it was even safe in the first place)? Should I consider pursuing other fields?

Something I like to tell myself: “Machines will never be able to fully grasp a literary text’s meaning, its artistry, or its historical, cultural, and etymological context.”

“Maybe, maybe not,” I cynically respond. Imagine a Google translate of the future that allows settings for input and output. Translate one of Basho's haikus into “teenage German vernacular.” Translate Romeo and Juliet into “Latin, in the style of Cicero.” Translate Kafka’s Metamorphosis to English using predominantly Anglo-Saxon words. What if one day Machine Translation has this ability to alter its algorithms to include stylistic input and output? I don’t think this is impossible. After all, Google Translate learns from its usage. The more translations that are run through it, the stronger it gets, and the smarter the translators get, the smarter it gets. With the right upkeep, it will always have more memory, more resources, more algorithms than its human counterparts.

Many translators, including myself, sometimes use Google Translate for reference, and after getting a rough draft of an excerpt, they work from there to imbue the text with the elements “lost in translation.” If Machine Translation can help us translate more efficiently, without completely taking over our careers, we should celebrate its consequence: the increase of world literature available in English. As translators, isn’t that our ultimate goal?

So what does the future look like for translators? Will we become obsolete (or when?)? Will we have to pursue careers managing and coding Translation Machines? I can’t say. For now, Google Translate is unable to capture style, rhyme, and rhythm; it can only reproduce meaning. For now, Translation Machines are unable to adequately compute the artistic qualities of texts. For now, human translation prevails.

Maybe one day Translation Machines will be able to interpret a text’s beauty. Until then, we need tech-savvy human translators working on world literature. Until then, we need innovative and passionate translators to do what Machines cannot. Until then, I’ll be translating.
